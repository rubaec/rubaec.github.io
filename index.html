Research

I’ve been into cryptography since I was a kid, scribbling secret ciphers and writing encryption programs for fun. Now I work on password-based protocols, spam prevention in messaging apps, and threshold signatures—focusing on where real-world usability meets strong security. I'm especially interested in making privacy-preserving tech that people can actually use.

I’ve been fascinated by cryptography since I was a kid; drawing up my own ciphers in elementary school and hiding secret alphabets in empty pen cartridges. My first real program in Java was an encryption scheme using multi-alphabet substitution.
When I read a book on cryptography, I thought cryptography was already “solved” as we already had symmetric and asymmetric encryption. What else could there be? Turns out: a lot.
When I started studying computer science, I took every crypto lecture I could. I dug into papers on Google Scholar in my free time and even found two attacks on a proposed scheme. That led to my own secure protocol and eventually became my master’s thesis.
Now, as a PhD student, I work on cryptographic protocols—especially password-based authentication. It’s a fun area where real-world usability meets deep theory. Passwords are weak (we all know it), but people still use them. Changing the human is an utopia, so the question becomes: how can we build secure systems around that, without forcing users to change their behavior?
I’m also working on defenses against spam in messaging apps like Signal, and on threshold Schnorr signatures. At the end of the day, I believe security and usability should go hand in hand. A perfect crypto system doesn’t help anyone if it’s too hard to use.

Research

My research focuses on protocols that balance strong security with real-world usability. Currently, I work on password-based authentication schemes, where users rely on low-entropy secrets, requiring careful design to resist brute-force attacks without compromising user experience. I also work on other cryptographic primitives used in practice, such as threshold Schnorr signatures and defenses against spam in privacy-preserving messaging protocols. My broader goal is to develop cryptographic tools that are not only provably secure, but also practical and widely adoptable.
